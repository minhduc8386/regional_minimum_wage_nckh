## Initial Synthesis

The literature is methodologically diverse. Classic minimum-wage studies rely heavily on quasi-experimental comparisons, fixed effects, DiD, border discontinuities, and event-study logic. Developing-country studies add a central complication: minimum wages may affect not only employment levels but the allocation of workers between formal jobs, informal wage work, self-employment, unemployment, and inactivity. Vietnam studies already show evidence on formal employment, wage employment, self-employment, wages, welfare, working hours, and firm-size heterogeneity. However, current evidence is still relatively thin on recent province/district-level informal employment outcomes and on flexible non-linear nuisance relationships, which is where DML and HTE tools may contribute if the data support them.
